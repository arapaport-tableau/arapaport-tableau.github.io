A Client Doesn't Announce When They're Leaving

What I built for Tableau Conference — and why it's the system we were still trying to solve for when I left PNC in 2021.

Andy Rapaport
Principal Solution Engineer · Salesforce
May 2026
7 min read

Marcus Johnson manages a commercial portfolio with about three dozen clients. Across manufacturing, logistics, healthcare — the normal mix you'd expect at a regional bank. He's a good RM and clients trust him.

One of those clients is in the process of leaving and he doesn't know it yet.

In the demo I used at Tableau Conference this year, the client was called Coastal Logistics. It's a fictional company, but this situation is something that happens in real life. Deposits were down around 34% over six months. Treasury management activity had fallen off pretty sharply too — ACH and wire volume mostly. The CFO hadn't heard from Marcus in almost three months.

Nobody missed it because they were bad at their job. The information just lived in different places and nobody was really connecting it together in a way that surfaced early enough to matter. Some of it was in the core deposit platform. Some in CRM. Some in other transactional and billing systems. There probably was a report somewhere that would've shown it if somebody had gone looking.

That's kind of the point.

Before Salesforce I spent 11 years at PNC, and a decent amount of that time was tied to portfolio analytics and other data driven initiatives. RM scorecards, relationship health reporting, that sort of thing. We were actually pretty good at identifying early warning signs. The hard part was operationalizing any of it.

You'd build the dashboard and socialize it to the end users. Then you'd realize the entire process still depended on somebody remembering to open it at the exact right moment.

Most of the time they didn't. Or they were busy. Or the signal got buried under fifteen other priorities that day.

So when we started building the Sentinel demo for Tableau Conference, the thing I cared about wasn't really the visualization layer. It was whether the system could take on the responsibility of noticing in the first place.

Under the hood it wasn't actually that complicated.

Sentinel runs overnight against the commercial portfolio looking at deposit behavior, treasury activity, product usage, recent engagement history — basically the relationship signals banks already track today, just tied together through a semantic layer. If something crosses a threshold, the system generates an alert and pushes it directly into Slack.

So Marcus walks in Monday morning and the issue is already waiting for him. An outreach email has already been drafted on his behalf and a call prep document has also been generated with data queried from the same semantic model.

That part landed with the audience well enough, but honestly the thing people kept asking about afterward was the manager workflow.

The RM's manager gets their own summary each week showing unresolved attrition risk across the team, how long alerts have been sitting untouched, estimated revenue exposure, things like that. One of the scenarios showed an RM sitting on about $840K of exposed revenue for several days without taking action.

From there the escalation process was automatic. Create the task. Notify the RM. Log the activity back into Salesforce. If the manager wanted more detail on a client, they could ask the Agentforce agent directly in Slack and get back charts and supporting context instead of just text summaries.

What people reacted to wasn't really the AI piece by itself. Banks have had predictive models for years. The interesting part was closing the loop operationally and actually tracking whether anybody followed through.

I've been describing it internally as agentic analytics, although I'm not sure anybody loves that term anymore. The basic idea is just that the system doesn't sit around waiting for somebody to think of the right question. It monitors the portfolio continuously, decides what matters, and pushes the issue toward action.

The RM still has to decide what to do with it. Nobody's removing that judgment layer.

A couple of years ago building something like this would've been messy. You would've had separate workflows stitched together with custom integration work everywhere. What's changed recently is that the platform pieces finally exist in one place — Tableau Next, Data 360, Agentforce, Slack, Einstein — without needing to glue everything together manually.

I wasn't completely sure the fictional RM framing would work on stage. It felt a little different from the normal conference demo setup. But people recognized the situation immediately because most commercial bankers have seen some version of it before: a client slowly disengaging while all the signals technically already existed somewhere in the data.

That's really what Sentinel was trying to solve.

The data lives in Data 360. In a real deployment you'd be pulling from a combination of Salesforce CRM and external platforms — core banking, treasury management, billing — ideally through Zero Copy so you're not maintaining duplicate copies of data that already lives somewhere else.

The risk scoring itself isn't something Sentinel is doing. The assumption is that a data science team has already built that model — it outputs attrition risk scores for each relationship and writes them to a table in the data warehouse. The semantic layer picks those up and enriches them with engagement history, transaction trends, contact frequency. That combined view is what everything downstream queries against.

The overnight scan is a scheduled Agentforce action. It fires a Flow that has a query embedded in an Apex class, which runs against the semantic model. When a client crosses the risk threshold, the agent triggers another Flow that takes the query output, formats it, and pushes it to Slack as the alert the RM sees in the morning.

The outreach email and call prep document are Einstein Prompt templates — both querying the semantic model directly. No separate lookups, no additional data sources. The prompt gets the client context and generates the content from it.

The manager escalation is the same pattern. Scheduled action, Flow, query against the semantic model — but instead of individual client alerts it's rolling up unresolved risk across the team. Anything that's been sitting too long escalates automatically.

The whole thing is more orchestration than AI. The intelligence is in the scoring model the data science team built. Agentforce and the Flows are just the delivery mechanism — making sure the output actually reaches the person who needs to act on it.
