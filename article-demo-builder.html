How I Cut Demo Prep Time from 2 Weeks to 2 Hours

Building a good Tableau Next demo used to take two weeks. Here's how I automated the whole pipeline — and what became possible when I did.

Andy Rapaport
Principal Solution Engineer · Salesforce
May 2026
6 min read

This whole thing started because I got tired of staying up late at night to manually sign my kids up for childcare through my athletic club's website so that I could play tennis.

They release the spots at a specific time each week and it's first-come, first-served. If you miss it, you're probably not getting a spot. For a while I was staying up late refreshing the page trying to time it right. Eventually I got annoyed enough to automate it.

I used Claude Code for most of it. The app logged in, watched for openings, submitted the reservation, texted me if something failed. Nothing especially sophisticated. It worked though. My kids got their spots and I stopped thinking about it.

At the time I mostly treated it like a side project.

I'm a Principal Solutions Engineer at Salesforce focused on Tableau. A big part of the job is building demos that feel believable to customers — not generic product tours, but something close enough to their actual business that they can picture themselves using it. In banking especially, details matter. A generic dashboard usually doesn't land very well.

The issue was that Tableau Next demos took a huge amount of work to build properly.

You'd start with the story you wanted to tell based on discovery done with the customer. Then generate synthetic data that actually supported that story. Then engineer realistic signals into the data so the outputs didn't feel fake. After that you'd deploy everything into Tableau Cloud, build the semantic model, create visualizations, assemble dashboards, configure Concierge, wire up the workflows around it.

There were a lot of moving pieces.

A full build could easily take two weeks or more depending on complexity. Which meant most of us naturally narrowed scope. Put together a dashboard, maybe show some Concierge functionality, call it good enough and move on to the next opportunity.

The problem is Tableau Next is actually much more interesting when all the pieces work together. We just usually didn't have enough time to build demos that way.

After the childcare app I started paying closer attention to where else I was spending time on repetitive setup work.

The first thing I built was a Tableau Pulse demo generator. You described the scenario in plain English and it generated synthetic data, metrics and groups to follow those metrics. That worked well enough that I kept extending it.

A few weeks later — after a fair amount of trial and error, API debugging, and figuring out undocumented behavior by testing things repeatedly — I had a Tableau Next demo builder that could handle most of the pipeline end to end.

Story setup. Data generation. Signal engineering. Tableau Cloud deployment. Semantic model creation. Dashboard generation. Concierge configuration.

Once the setup time dropped to a few hours, the calculation changed pretty quickly.

That's how Sentinel happened.

Sentinel was the demo I presented at Tableau Conference this year. Commercial banking portfolio monitoring, basically. The system runs overnight against a portfolio looking for attrition indicators — declining deposits, treasury management dropoff, reduced engagement activity, things like that. If a client starts showing signs of risk, the RM gets alerted directly in Slack along with generated outreach content and supporting context.

If the alert sits unresolved too long, it escalates to management automatically.

Five platforms working together. The agent runs overnight, identifies at-risk clients, drafts outreach, and surfaces alerts — without anyone having to go looking for the problem.

I probably wouldn't have attempted something at that level of complexity before building the demo tooling. There were too many interconnected parts and too much setup overhead to justify experimenting with it.

The interesting thing is that none of this really required me to become a software engineer.

Before the childcare project I had essentially no programming background. What I did have was enough domain experience to know what I wanted the system to do and enough context to recognize when the outputs were wrong or unrealistic.

That's honestly most of the job with these coding models.

Claude was very good at generating implementation paths once the requirements were clear. The harder part was usually steering — explaining the business logic, validating the behavior, correcting edge cases, refining the workflows, figuring out where the generated output didn't actually align with how Tableau or banking systems work in practice.

The Tableau Next Demo Builder ended up open source mostly because other SEs kept asking about it after seeing the demos internally.

I don't really think the specific implementation matters that much. Mine works for how I build demos. Someone else will almost certainly build a better version.

But I do think every Solutions Engineer working with platforms like Tableau is probably going to end up with some version of this workflow eventually. The amount of manual scaffolding involved in demo building was always a bottleneck. AI just made it a solvable one.
