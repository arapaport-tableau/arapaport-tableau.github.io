Two Weeks to Two Hours

Building enterprise solutions used to mean two weeks of scaffolding for every idea worth showing. AI compressed that to a couple of hours and changed where I spend my time.

Andy Rapaport
Principal Solution Engineer · Salesforce
May 2026
7 min read

This started because I got tired of staying up late to sign my kids up for childcare at my athletic club so I could play tennis.

They release the spots at a specific time each week. First-come, first-served. If you miss it, you're probably not getting a spot. For a while I was refreshing the page at odd hours trying to time it right. Eventually I got annoyed enough to automate it.

I used Claude Code. The app logged in, watched for openings, submitted the reservation, texted me if something failed. Nothing sophisticated. It worked though. My kids got their spots and I stopped thinking about it.

At the time I mostly treated it like a side project. But it changed how I thought about my actual job.

The setup tax

I'm a Solutions Engineer at Salesforce focused on Tableau and financial services. A big part of the job is building solutions that feel believable to customers. Not generic product tours, but something close enough to their actual business that they can picture themselves using it. In banking, details matter. If the data looks fake or the scenario feels generic, you lose the room.

The issue was that building a good solution always took a massive amount of setup work. You'd start with the story you wanted to tell based on discovery with the customer. Then generate synthetic data that supported that story. Then engineer realistic signals into the data so the outputs didn't feel contrived. After that you'd deploy everything, build the data model, create visualizations, configure AI components, wire up the workflows.

A full build could take two weeks or more depending on complexity. Which meant most of us naturally narrowed scope. Show one piece of the platform well, call it good enough, move on to the next opportunity.

The problem is that enterprise platforms are most compelling when all the pieces work together. We just rarely had time to build solutions that way.

The constraint was never the idea. It was the hours of scaffolding between the idea and the thing you actually wanted to show.

What changed

After the childcare project I started paying closer attention to where I was spending time on repetitive setup work. Data generation. Deployment. Configuration. The kind of work that required domain knowledge to get right but wasn't itself creative or strategic.

I started building automation around it. Piece by piece, over a few weeks. Each piece handled one part of the pipeline that used to be manual. Eventually I had something that could take a plain-English description of a scenario and produce a working solution in a couple of hours instead of two weeks.

To be clear, the automation doesn't replace the platform. It handles the setup work of building on top of it. The governance, the security model, the semantic layer - that's still Tableau and Salesforce doing what they do. I just stopped spending two weeks configuring it by hand.

The important thing is what that made possible. When setup time drops from two weeks to two hours, the math changes. You stop narrowing scope. You start attempting things you wouldn't have considered before because the cost of trying was too high.

What it enabled

I built a demo for Tableau Conference this year that I probably wouldn't have attempted otherwise. It was a commercial banking portfolio monitoring system. An agent runs overnight against a client portfolio looking for attrition indicators: declining deposits, reduced engagement, treasury management dropoff. If a client shows signs of risk, the relationship manager gets alerted in Slack along with generated outreach content and supporting context. If the alert sits too long, it escalates to management.

Five platforms working together. Data layer, analytics, agent orchestration, AI-generated content, messaging. The kind of thing where any one piece is straightforward but the integration complexity makes the whole thing impractical to build manually for a single engagement.

With the setup time removed from the equation, I could focus entirely on the story and the architecture. That's a different kind of work. And honestly, it's a more valuable kind of work.

AI didn't eliminate the work. It moved me from implementation to architecture. The domain knowledge still matters. The judgment about what to build and why still matters. What changed is where I spend my time.

The broader pattern

I don't think this is specific to solutions engineering. Anyone who does knowledge work that involves repetitive setup or configuration is looking at the same shift. The work doesn't disappear. But the ratio between setup and thinking changes a lot.

Before, my time split was probably 80% implementation and 20% strategy. Now it's closer to the reverse. I spend most of my time on discovery, architecture, storytelling. The part that requires 11 years of banking experience. The part that AI is bad at because it doesn't know what a CFO actually cares about or what makes a banker lean forward in a meeting.

When people ask whether AI replaces their job, I think the honest answer is: it replaces the scaffolding around the judgment. The judgment itself still has to come from somewhere. And once the scaffolding is gone, you find out pretty quickly whether the judgment was the bottleneck or the setup was.

What I didn't expect

I had essentially no programming background before the childcare project. What I had was enough domain experience to know what I wanted the system to do and enough context to recognize when the outputs were wrong.

That turned out to be most of the job. Claude was good at generating implementation paths once the requirements were clear. The harder part was steering. Explaining the business logic. Validating behavior. Correcting edge cases. Figuring out where the output didn't align with how banking systems actually work in practice.

The domain expertise turned out to be the thing that mattered, not the coding ability. I did not expect that going in.

I think most practitioners working on enterprise platforms will end up somewhere similar. The setup overhead was always the constraint. Now it's not. What you do with the time you get back is the interesting part, and I'm still figuring that out.
